## 1. Introduction

This report presents the design and simulated execution of an A/B testing experiment using an integrated **Retail Sales and Customer Insights dataset**.

The objective of this experiment is to move beyond descriptive analytics and implement a controlled testing framework that enables data-driven decision-making. Specifically, the experiment evaluates whether promotional incentives positively influence customer purchasing behavior.

---

## 2. Business Goal

### Problem Statement

The business is experiencing **inconsistent customer purchasing behavior**, characterized by:

- Irregular purchase frequency  
- Early customer drop-off  
- Unstable revenue growth  
- Reduced customer retention  

This inconsistency limits predictable revenue scaling and long-term growth planning.

### Objective

To determine whether **promotional incentives** increase:

- Customer purchase frequency  
- Total customer spending  

### Definition of Success

A successful test will provide statistically supported evidence that customers exposed to promotional incentives demonstrate improved purchasing behavior compared to those under standard shopping conditions.

If validated, the promotional strategy can be scaled to drive predictable revenue growth and improved retention.

---

## 3. Formal Hypotheses

The experiment evaluates the impact of promotional treatment using the following hypotheses:

### Null Hypothesis (H₀)

There is **no statistically significant difference** in purchasing behavior between customers who receive promotional incentives and those who do not.

> Promotions do not increase purchase frequency or total spending.

### Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

Customers receiving promotional incentives demonstrate **higher purchase frequency and total spending** compared to customers who do not receive promotions.

If measurable improvement is observed in the treatment group, the null hypothesis will be rejected.

---

## 4. Primary Metric (KPI)

### Selected KPI: **Purchase Frequency per Customer**

**Definition:**  
The average number of purchases made by a customer during the experiment period.

### Rationale

Purchase frequency was selected because it:

- Directly reflects customer engagement  
- Measures repeat buying behavior  
- Serves as a leading indicator of long-term revenue growth  
- Is highly actionable for marketing strategy decisions  

### Winning Criteria

If the treatment group shows a **higher average purchase frequency per customer** than the control group, the test will be considered successful.

This KPI serves as the primary decision-making metric.

---

## 5. Audience Segmentation & Experimental Split

### Selected Dimension: **Customer Age Group**

Customers were segmented by age group to ensure fair comparison across similar behavioral cohorts.

### Group Structure

| Group | Description | Promotional Exposure |
|--------|------------|----------------------|
| **Group A (Control)** | Customers across selected age groups | No promotional incentives |
| **Group B (Treatment)** | Customers from the same age groups | Received promotional incentives |

### Justification

- Ensures demographic balance  
- Reduces behavioral bias  
- Maintains comparability between groups  
- Allows performance differences to be attributed solely to treatment  

### Alignment With Business Goal

Testing across age groups ensures that promotional impact is evaluated across varying customer behaviors, enabling scalable and reliable strategic decisions.

---

## 6. Treatment Definition

### Treatment Applied to Group B

Customers in **Group B** received:

- Targeted discounts  
- Special purchase offers  
- Promotional incentives designed to increase repeat purchases  

### Control Condition (Group A)

Customers in **Group A** continued under standard shopping conditions without any promotional changes.

### Measurable Impact Variables

The treatment effect was evaluated using:

- Purchase frequency  
- Quantity purchased  
- Total revenue generated  

Improvement in Group B relative to Group A indicates positive treatment impact.

---

## 7. Experimental Design Overview

| Component | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Experiment Type | Randomized A/B Test |
| Dataset | Retail Sales & Customer Insights |
| Total Customers | 200 |
| Control Group Size | 101 |
| Treatment Group Size | 99 |
| Primary KPI | Purchase Frequency per Customer |
| Secondary Metrics | Quantity Purchased, Total Revenue |

Random assignment ensured group equivalency and minimized confounding variables.

---

## 8. Experiment Results

### Group Distribution

| Metric | Group A (Control) | Group B (Treatment) |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Customers | 101 | 99 |
| Revenue | 7.73 Million | 7.74 Million |
| Quantity Purchased | ~7.4 Thousand Units | ~7.5 Thousand Units |

### Overall Performance Metrics

| Metric | Value |
|----------|--------|
| Average Purchase Frequency per Customer | ~25 purchases |
| Average Quantity per Customer | ~74.75 units |
| Total Combined Revenue | 15.48 Million |

### Key Observations

- Group sizes were nearly equal, ensuring valid comparison.  
- Group B generated slightly higher revenue than Group A.  
- Quantity purchased was marginally higher in Group B.  
- Treatment group revenue was approximately **0.13% higher** than control.

### 9. Result Interpretation

The treatment group demonstrated **marginal improvements** across revenue and quantity metrics compared to the control group.

### Interpretation Summary

- Promotional incentives show **positive directional impact**.
- Performance gains are measurable but small.
- Statistical significance testing would be required for formal validation.
- The limited magnitude suggests further optimization is necessary.

### Business Insight

While the improvement is modest, the results indicate that promotional strategies may influence customer behavior. However, refinement of:

- Offer structure  
- Discount size  
- Targeting precision  
- Campaign timing  

may be required to achieve stronger business impact.

---

## 10. Conclusion & Business Implications

This A/B testing framework enables the organization to evaluate promotional strategies using controlled experimentation rather than assumptions.

### Strategic Value

- Introduces scientific decision-making into marketing strategy  
- Enables scalable revenue optimization  
- Supports customer retention improvement initiatives  
- Establishes a repeatable experimentation model  

### Final Assessment

The promotional treatment produced a **small but positive effect** on purchasing behavior. While not transformational, the results justify:

1. Further experimentation  
2. Treatment optimization  
3. Statistical validation with larger samples  

The organization is now positioned to refine promotional strategies using data-backed insights, improving long-term engagement and revenue stability.
